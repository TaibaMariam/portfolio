Journal / Service Notes

Chickpea & Rice Soup — Consistency Is a Skill

At first glance, chickpea and rice soup looks simple. It doesn't rely on expensive ingredients, complicated techniques, or decorative plating. But that simplicity is exactly what makes it demanding. In a professional kitchen, a dish like this tests consistency more than creativity.

When preparing this soup during daily service, I learned quickly that every step matters. The rice has to be cooked just right — not overcooked, not chalky — because its texture affects the entire bowl. Chickpeas need to be tender without breaking apart. The broth must hold body without being heavy, and seasoning needs constant attention because it changes as the soup rests and reheats during service.

What challenged me most was maintaining the same standard throughout service. The first bowl and the last bowl must taste the same. That requires tasting repeatedly, adjusting seasoning carefully, and being aware of temperature at all times. A soup that cools too much loses its balance; a soup held too hot can lose texture.

This dish taught me that repetition is not boring — it is how skill is built. Making the same soup multiple times forces you to notice small differences and correct them before they become mistakes. It also taught me patience and discipline. There is no shortcut to consistency.

In many ways, this soup represents what I am working toward as a chef. Not chasing complexity, but mastering the basics. Understanding how ingredients behave, respecting process, and delivering the same quality every time, even during busy service.

Simple dishes reveal how well a kitchen really functions. Learning to execute them properly has been one of the most valuable parts of my daily service experience.

Tomato Salad — Balance Over Decoration

A tomato salad does not hide mistakes. There is no sauce to cover imbalance, no garnish to distract from poor seasoning, and no complex technique to excuse inconsistency. It stands on balance alone.

Working with this dish during service taught me how much attention even cold starters require. Tomato selection matters — ripeness, firmness, and acidity all affect the final result. Knife work matters because uneven slices affect both texture and presentation. Even the order of seasoning makes a difference: salt too early and the tomatoes release too much water; too late and the flavor doesn't integrate properly.

What I learned most from this dish was restraint. It is tempting to add more — more dressing, more herbs, more oil — but the best result often comes from stopping at the right moment. Balance is not about intensity, but harmony.

During service, this salad also reinforced the importance of clean plating. Cold dishes reach the guest exactly as they leave the pass. There is no heat to distract from messy edges or inconsistent portioning. Every plate must look intentional.

This dish helped me understand that professionalism in the kitchen is not about making things complicated. It is about knowing when something is complete. Knowing when to stop adjusting. Knowing that simplicity demands confidence.

As I continue developing as a chef, this mindset stays with me. Respect the ingredients. Taste carefully. Plate cleanly. Let the dish speak for itself.

One Dish, One Standard — What Daily Service Is Teaching Me

Every day in a professional kitchen reinforces the same lesson: improvement comes from standards, not from single moments of creativity. One good service does not make a good chef. Consistent standards do.

Whether I am preparing soups, cold starters, or assisting with desserts, I focus on one main question: What standard am I practicing today? It might be cleanliness of my station, speed without rushing, or maintaining calm when service pressure increases.

Daily service teaches you to respect process. Mise en place is not just preparation; it is responsibility. If something is missing or poorly prepared, the whole team feels it. I have learned to double-check my work, anticipate needs, and communicate clearly, even when the kitchen is busy.

One of the most valuable lessons for me has been understanding pace. Moving fast does not mean moving carelessly. It means being organized enough that speed feels controlled. This is something I am still improving, and I approach it deliberately rather than trying to rush progress.

I am not yet where I want to be — and that honesty matters. I am still learning, still refining my technique, still building confidence through repetition. But I am serious about the path I am on. I show up prepared, open to feedback, and focused on doing the work properly.

For me, becoming a better chef is not about titles. It is about habits. And daily service, with all its pressure and repetition, is where those habits are built.
